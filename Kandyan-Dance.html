Kandyan Dance

Kandyan dance is a classical dance form from Sri Lanka, native to the central hills region (Kandy). Originating from an ancient healing and exorcism ritual called the Kohomba Kankariya, it is known for its powerful, dynamic movements, often performed by males in elaborate costumes. It has been heavily influenced by both indigenous and South Indian dance traditions.

Ceremonial and acrobatic, Kandyan dance is known for precise footwork, jumps, spins, and graceful movements. It is performed at festivals like Esala Perahera.

Kandyan dances were traditionally taught by gurus and guru lineages. This system allowed some variations in the technique. However, since the mid-1950s a standardised teaching method has been formulated. It includes twelve foot bar exercises for the feet, and twelve exercises without the bar.

Kandyan dance is known for its strength and dynamism, traditionally recognized as a highly masculine art form, though today it is performed by both men and women. The style is marked by powerful leaps, rapid spins, and intricate footwork that demand both energy and precision from the dancer.

The costume is another defining feature, especially for male performers who wear a distinctive skirt-like garment along with ornate silver ornaments and an impressive headdress that enhances the grandeur of the performance. The dance is accompanied mainly by rhythmic drumming, with the Geta Beraya being the primary instrument, while cymbals such as the Thalampota are sometimes added to enrich the rhythm.

Many movements in Kandyan dance draw inspiration from nature and legend. Dancers often imitate animals like elephants or peacocks, and they also portray episodes from the Ramayana or retell stories of ancient kings and heroic figures, making the performance both expressive and narrative.
